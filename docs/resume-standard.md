## About this material

This material consolidates the **CV Camaleão** methodology, presented as part of the GO
GLOBAL method from Borderless Coding. Its central goal is to transform how Brazilian
developers communicate their professional value in resumes aimed at international roles.
The content spans from diagnosing the problem (rejection in 6 seconds) to the practical
delivery of the artifacts needed to compete for global positions.

---

## 1. The real problem — rejection in 6 seconds

International recruiters spend on average 6 to 7 seconds on the first screening of a
resume. In that window the decision is binary: the candidate is classified as a **cheap
executor** (a generic developer who lists technologies without demonstrating real impact)
or as a **global professional with real value** (an engineer who communicates measurable
results, shows technical maturity, and positions themselves as a solver of complex
problems).

The difference between these two profiles is not technical competence. It is **value
communication**. Most Brazilian developers never get a callback from international roles
not for lack of ability, but for a failure of presentation.

---

## 2. The 6-second test — 3 resume patterns

Three resume archetypes illustrate the quality spectrum:

- **Generic BR resume:** Traditional Brazilian format, full of text, no metrics, with a
  photo, generic responsibilities listed. Reads as a junior executor.
- **Average resume:** Reasonable structure, attempts to show results, but without concrete
  numbers or precise technical language. Goes unnoticed.
- **Camaleão-standard resume:** Clean, strong headline, evident metrics, precise technical
  language, impact storytelling. Communicates authority and value from the first glance.

The central question: if the recruiter is paying USD 80K–150K/year, which of these
professionals would they call for an interview?

## 4. What the CV Camaleão is

The CV Camaleão is not a pretty template or a set of canned phrases. It is a **dynamic,
replicable structure** that adapts strategically to three dimensions of the global market:

- **To the specific job:** Exact keywords from the job description, skills prioritized by
  relevance, achievements aligned with the challenges mentioned.
- **To the country/region:** Appropriate ATS format, explicit time zones, compliance with
  cultural norms (no photo for US/UK, European format when needed).
- **To the level of the opportunity:** Technical language adjusted (junior vs. senior),
  depth of the cases presented, complexity of the systems described.

The adaptation is in the presentation of value, not in fabricating competencies you do not
have. You do not invent experiences — you learn to communicate the real ones
strategically.

## 5. What changes with the CV Camaleão — transformation

The fundamental change is in **how you communicate what you do**. The global market does
not hire developers who know technologies. It hires engineers who solve complex problems
and produce measurable results.

Critical transformations:

- From "listing frameworks" to **showing the impact of technical decisions**. Instead of
  "Experience with React, Node.js, AWS", write something like "Architected React-based
  dashboard reducing load time by 73%, improving user retention by 40%".
- From "sending a resume" to **sending a clear value proposition** — each resume
  customized to demonstrate exactly how you solve the specific challenges of that job.
- From "looking like an anxious junior" to **communicating global maturity** — precise
  technical language, scale metrics, demonstration of autonomy and ownership.
- From "pretty design without substance" to **technical language + real metrics** — a
  clean, professional format that prioritizes impactful content over empty aesthetics.

## 6. The structure — 7 sections of the CV Camaleão

The CV Camaleão follows a specific architecture with a sequence optimized to capture
attention in the first 6 seconds. Each section has a strategic purpose:

**01 — Header (the first impression decides everything):** Professional headline, location
with time zone, remote-ready, working links. This is where you win or lose the recruiter.

**02 — Summary (your pitch in 4 lines):** Technical specialties + at least 2 impact metrics
+ next career objective. Dense text, zero fluff.

**03 — Key Achievements (immediate social proof):** Your 3 biggest measurable results. The
recruiter needs to see value in the first 10 seconds.

**04 — Experience (show impact, not tasks):** Each position with company context + 3–6
bullets following the STAR-M framework. You tell the story of the problem you solved.

**05 — Projects (be a developer with living proof):** 2–4 relevant projects with working
links, a clear tech stack, and quantified results. Especially critical for those seeking a
first international role.

**06 — Skills (group, don't pile up):** 5 thematic blocks (Languages, Frameworks, Cloud,
Tools, Soft Skills) with 20–25 skills in total. Organization demonstrates maturity.

**07 — Education & Certifications (last, always):** In the international market, experience
and results speak louder than a diploma. Education goes at the end, in a minimalist format.

Recruiters read top to bottom and stop when they lose interest. Placing impact
(Achievements) before detailed experience, and education at the end, is a deliberate
strategy.

## 7. Section 2 — Summary: your pitch in 4 lines

The Camaleão Summary rule:

- **Line 1:** Years of experience + area of specialization + main stack.
- **Lines 2–3:** At least 2 concrete metrics of technical impact you delivered.
- **Line 4:** A specific career objective aligned with the job.

The summary must be **dense**. No filler phrases, no motivational clichés, no empty
adjectives. Only technical facts and measurable results.

Examples that kill credibility and must be eliminated completely: "Passionate developer
eager to learn new technologies", "Team player with strong communication skills",
"Detail-oriented professional seeking new challenges", "Fast learner who adapts quickly to
new environments". These clichés communicate **inexperience and insecurity**. No senior
engineer writes this way.

Additional rule: always write the resume in English, even if the job is for a Brazilian
company that accepts Portuguese. This positions you as a global professional from the
start.

---

## 8. Section 3 — Key Achievements: immediate social proof

3 bullets. Strong results. Concrete numbers.

This section is the **biggest competitive differentiator**. While 95% of developers list
responsibilities, you show measurable achievements right at the top of the resume.

Structure of each Achievement: **action verb** (Enhanced, Built, Optimized) + **what you
did** (API performance, scheduling system) + **numeric result** (65% faster, 100k
events/day) + **optional technical context** (Redis caching, AWS EventBridge).

Keep each bullet to 1–2 lines maximum. Conciseness demonstrates clarity of thought.

Where do the numbers come from? From your real experiences. You just need to **measure and
translate**. Common sources: monitoring logs (Grafana, Datadog, New Relic), performance
metrics before/after optimizations, transaction/request volume, time reduction in
processes, cost savings (cloud bills, infrastructure), users affected by the improvements.

---

## 9. Section 4 — Experience: impact, not a task list

Anatomy of a well-written experience:

- **Line 1:** Company | Title | Location | Period (MM/YYYY format).
- **Line 2:** 1 sentence of context about the company (size, sector, relevance). This helps
  the recruiter calibrate the complexity of your challenges.
- **Bullets:** 3 to 6 bullets per position, each following the STAR-M framework. Prioritize
  the ones most relevant to the job you are applying for.

Critical formatting rules: reverse-chronological order (most recent first), at most 3–4
experiences (group the older ones or leave out the less relevant), each bullet starts with
a Power Verb (never "Responsible for" or "Worked on"), numbers whenever possible (use
honest estimates if needed), tech stack integrated naturally into the context.

Old experiences (5+ years) can be reduced to 1–2 bullets each or grouped into an "Earlier
Experience" block at the end. Focus the space on recent, relevant experiences.

## 10. The STAR-M framework — turning tasks into impact

STAR-M is the structure that separates a generic bullet from a bullet that sells. Each
letter represents an essential component:

| Letter | Meaning | What to write |
| --- | --- | --- |
| S | Situation | Context of the problem or challenge you faced |
| T | Task | Tech stack used and the technical approach chosen |
| A | Action | What specifically you implemented or developed |
| R | Result | Quantified result (%, time, cost, users impacted) |
| M | Magnitude | Scale of the impact (transaction volume, uptime, users, requests) |

In practice you do not need to include all 5 elements in every bullet, but you need at
least **Action + Result**, ideally with **Magnitude**. The more elements you include
naturally, the stronger the bullet.

Valid bullet combinations:

- **Tech + Result + Scale:** "Built Python data pipeline (Apache Airflow) processing 5M
  records/day, reducing ETL time by 60%."
- **Problem + Action + Impact:** "Resolved critical memory leak in React app causing
  crashes for 20% of users through component optimization."
- **Context + Tech + Outcome:** "Migrated monolith to microservices (Docker/Kubernetes)
  enabling independent deployments and 99.95% uptime."

## 11. Power Verbs — never freeze writing bullets again

Golden rule: never start with "Responsible for" or "Worked on".

Power Verbs are specific action verbs that communicate ownership, impact, and technical
leadership. They transform the recruiter's perception of your seniority and autonomy.

| Category | Power Verbs |
| --- | --- |
| Construction | Architected, Engineered, Implemented, Developed, Built, Designed, Created, Established |
| Performance | Optimized, Accelerated, Streamlined, Enhanced, Improved, Reduced, Increased, Boosted |
| Leadership | Spearheaded, Drove, Championed, Led, Directed, Orchestrated, Coordinated, Pioneered |
| Problem-solving | Resolved, Eliminated, Debugged, Diagnosed, Mitigated, Fixed, Addressed, Troubleshot |
| Scale & transformation | Scaled, Migrated, Automated, Transformed, Refactored, Modernized, Consolidated |
| Analysis & strategy | Analyzed, Evaluated, Assessed, Investigated, Researched, Identified, Defined |
| Collaboration | Collaborated, Partnered, Mentored, Facilitated, Integrated, Aligned, Contributed |
| Delivery & deployment | Deployed, Delivered, Launched, Released, Shipped, Rolled out, Executed |

How to choose the right verb: for projects from scratch use Architected, Engineered, Built;
for improvements use Optimized, Enhanced, Improved; for critical problems use Resolved,
Eliminated, Mitigated; for growth use Scaled, Automated, Transformed.

Variety demonstrates sophistication. Do not use the same verb in every bullet.

## 12. How to build real metrics

You **have** metrics. You just never measured them formally or never noticed their value.
Every line of code you write produces measurable impact. What matters is **plausibility and
honesty**, not scientific precision.

4 proven techniques to create real metrics:

**1. Retroactive estimation:** Compare time/performance before and after your
implementation. Sources: git history with timestamps, deploy logs, your own documented
memory.

**2. Proxy metrics:** Use indirect indicators of your work's impact. Don't know how many
users? Use database records, active sessions, request volume per day. Sources: database row
counts, basic analytics, access logs, CloudWatch metrics.

**3. Scale of impact:** Quantify the reach of the system you built/improved. Example: "API
serving 50K daily active users" or "System processing 2M+ transactions/month". Sources: ask
the PM/PO, check user tables, analyze request logs.

**4. Temporal comparison:** Document the difference in the system's state before/after you.
Sources: issue trackers (Jira, Linear), git blame, sprint retrospectives.

If you did not measure exactly, use "~" or "approximately" before the number. This is
completely acceptable and infinitely better than zero metrics. Never invent completely
false numbers — always estimate based on real evidence.

## 13. ATS survival guide — before the human comes the machine

ATS (Applicant Tracking System) is the software that medium and large companies use to
filter resumes automatically. An estimated 75% of resumes are rejected by the ATS before
any human reads them. 98% of Fortune 500 companies use some kind of ATS.

**Always do:**

- Single-column format (double columns confuse the parser)
- Standard headers in English: use "Experience", "Education", "Skills"
- Save as PDF (Word can break depending on the version)
- Safe fonts: Arial, Calibri, Helvetica, Times New Roman
- Exact keywords from the job description (if the job asks for "React.js", write "React.js",
  not "React" or "ReactJS")
- Font size >= 10pt
- Simple bullets: use - or the default, not complex Unicode symbols

**Never do:**

- Complex tables (the ATS reads cell by cell and mixes information)
- Floating text boxes (content is frequently ignored by the parser)
- Headers/footers with critical data (put name and contact in the document body)
- Icons instead of text (a phone icon without the number written is invisible to the ATS)
- Skill graphics (proficiency bars are not read — list skills as text)
- Decorative or script fonts (OCR fails frequently with these fonts)
- Images with text (text inside images is not extracted)

## 14. CV Master vs. submitted CV

The Camaleão methodology works through two distinct artifacts:

**CV Master:** Your complete, comprehensive version, an internal reference document. A
repository of all your experiences, projects, achievements, and skills. Never sent. It can
be 3–4 pages, including old projects, all skills, certifications. Updated monthly or after
each significant project completed.

**Submitted CV (CV Enviado):** A version strategically adapted and optimized for the
specific job. A conversion tool to turn an application into a callback and an interview.
Always sent — each job gets a customized version. At most 2 pages, focused only on what is
relevant to that opportunity. Created from scratch for each application, from the Master.

What **changes** between the Master and each submitted CV: Summary (rewritten to use the
exact keywords from the job description), Skills (reorganized to put the required skills at
the top), Key Achievements (the 3 most relevant selected from all), Projects (the 2–3 with
the stack most similar to what the job asks for).

What **never changes:** Header (name, contact, base headline), the 7-section structure,
Education & Certifications, the chronological order of experiences, the general formatting
and layout.

## 15. Red flags that kill your resume

Mistakes that act as automatic rejection signals for international recruiters:

- **Photo on the resume:** A huge red flag in the US, Canada, UK, and Australia for
  discrimination reasons. Only in continental Europe (Germany, France) is it expected. When
  in doubt, leave it out.
- **"Responsible for...":** Passive language that communicates a junior executor. Always
  replaced with active Power Verbs.
- **40+ piled-up skills:** A disorganized list shows lack of curation. Organize into 5
  thematic blocks with 20–25 relevant skills in total.
- **Generic summary:** Motivational clichés kill credibility. Write with concrete metrics
  and clear technical specialization.
- **Dates in BR format:** 20/05/2024 confuses international recruiters (day or month
  first?). Always use MM/YYYY or "May 2024" for global clarity.
- **More than 2 pages:** For less than 10 years of experience, 2 pages is the maximum. Long
  resumes communicate a lack of synthesis and prioritization.
- **Excessive design:** Proficiency bars, pie charts for skills, overly vibrant colors,
  decorative icons. All of this hinders the ATS and looks unprofessional.
- **Broken/empty links:** GitHub with no relevant repositories, an incomplete LinkedIn, an
  offline portfolio. It is better to include no link than to include a link that
  disappoints.

Cultural context: what works in Brazil can be an international red flag and vice versa.
Always follow international standards if you want global roles.

## 16. Checklist — CV ready to ship

Before sending any application, go through this checklist. If you can mark 10/10, the
resume is at the global standard:

1. Headline with role + 3 clear technical specialties
2. Summary with at least 2 concrete impact metrics
3. 3 Key Achievements with numeric results
4. Each experience bullet follows the STAR-M framework (at least Action + Result +
   Magnitude)
5. Skills organized into 5 thematic blocks (20–25 total)
6. All links working and relevant (GitHub with real code, LinkedIn up to date)
7. Single-column and ATS-friendly format (no complex tables, text boxes, headers/footers
   with critical info; standard fonts Arial/Calibri; PDF)
8. Exact keywords from the job description present
9. Reviewed by someone fluent in native English (Grammarly Premium or a native speaker if
   possible)
10. At most 2 pages (for less than 10 years of experience)

Scoring: 10/10 = CV ready to ship. 8–9/10 = Almost there, minor adjustments. 6–7/10 = Needs
significant revision. Below 5/10 = Go back to the fundamentals.

## 17. Alignment with the GO GLOBAL method

The CV Camaleão is not an isolated tactic — it is one of the four pillars of Borderless
Coding's GO GLOBAL method:

- **Profile Package:** The Headline, Summary, and Links on the resume are the
  materialization of strategic positioning. What you define in the Profile Package must be
  reflected consistently in the resume. Result: alignment across LinkedIn, GitHub,
  Portfolio, and resume.
- **Interview Engine:** Each bullet written with STAR-M in the resume turns directly into a
  ready answer for a behavioral interview. Result: a natural transition from the resume to
  live storytelling.
- **Opportunity Pipeline:** A clean, ATS-friendly, adaptable format lets you apply to a
  high volume of jobs (10–15/week) while keeping quality. Result: a constant pipeline of
  interviews.
- **Senior Global Playbook:** Including time zones, "Remote Ready", a modern stack, and
  communication of scale/impact. Result: passing the maturity "smell test" for working with
  internationally distributed teams.

Developers who apply only the CV Camaleão in isolation see a 30–40% improvement in
callbacks. Developers who apply the full GO GLOBAL method (Profile + Interview + Pipeline +
Playbook) see a 200–300% improvement and land multiple simultaneous offers to negotiate.

## 18. Practical challenge — delivering the 3 artifacts

Knowledge without execution is just theory. The 3 artifacts of the CV Camaleão to deliver:

**Artifact 1 — Complete CV Camaleão (in English):** A complete structure with a Header with
a strong headline, a Summary with 2+ metrics, 3 Key Achievements, Experience with STAR-M
bullets, 2–4 relevant Projects with links, Skills organized into 5 blocks, Education. PDF
single-column format, at most 2 pages, standard fonts, ATS-friendly.

**Artifact 2 — Working, professional links:** GitHub with at least 3 relevant repositories
(clean code, descriptive READMEs), a fully filled-in LinkedIn aligned with the resume, a
Portfolio with 2–3 deployed projects (if applicable). All with descriptions in English.
Test: click each link as if you were a recruiter — they should all open and impress.

**Artifact 3 — "CV Ready to Ship" 10/10 checklist:** Print the checklist and mark each item
honestly. If you cannot reach 10/10, go back and adjust until you can. Do not send any
application before all 10 items are verified.
